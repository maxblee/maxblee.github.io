How have executions changed over time?

For almost every year since before the country was founded, the United States has executed citizens found guilty of crimes.


But the method in which people have been executed hasn't stayed the same.


Scroll below to explore the history of the death penalty in the United States. The data for this graph comes from the Epsy file of executions in the United States between 1608 and 2002 and the Death Penalty Information Center's database of executions between 1976 to 2019. The historical information comes from Death Penalty: An American History by Stuart Banner.


Throughout much of the 1800s, the predominant method of executions was hanging.


Hanging, at least at first, was a public spectacle meant to instruct people of the dangers of committing crimes. But over time, it became seen as a barbaric exercise.

By the late 1800s, people had begun seeking technological alternatives to hanging. Electrocution became a clear option; although the first death from commercial electricity was not recorded until 1881, an average of 40 people died each year between 1883 and 1888.


In 1890, the first person, William Kemmler, was executed by electrocution. Quickly, deaths by electrocution mounted, surpassing hangings as the predominant form of execution by 1920.

Other technological alternatives also emerged. Around the turn of the century, some people proposed lethal gas as an execution method; Nevada became the first state to adopt the practice in 1921. But gas never overtook electrocution as the dominant form of execution.

By the 1940s, the popularity of the death penalty as a whole was declining. Especially in the North, juries were sentencing people to death less often than they had previously, replacing death sentences with prison sentences.


Constitutional challenges to the death penalty, arguing that capital punishment was "cruel and unusual punishment" and violated the Eighth Amendment, cropped up as well. Finally, in 1972, amid a broader decline in executions, the Supreme Court declared in a 5-4 ruling that the death penalty was unconstitutional.

Opposition to the 1972 Furman v. Georgia ruling came quickly. States soon adopted various new sentencing schemes, hoping to get the Supreme Court to rule their adopted approaches Constitutional. Just three years after the case was decided, in 1975, 298 people were sentenced to death, the highest number for any year on record. And in the 1976 case Gregg v. Georgia, the Supreme Court affirmed sentencing schemes that gave juries some discretion in death penalty cases, causing executions to resume.

After Gregg v. Georgia, executions soon resumed and the method in which people were executed changed. Due to the hiatus from Furman v. Georgia, states had deteriorating infrastructure that made replacing their execution methods appear appealing financially. And lethal injection was seen as both cheap and, for the most part, painless.

Now, once again, executions are on the decline. Several states, including California, have declared a moratorium on the practice, with governors arguing that the death penalty is costly and immoral.

This analysis of executions in the United States comes from two sources, the Epsy File of executions in the United States from 1608 to 2002 and the Death Penalty Information Center data on executions from 1976 to present. In both cases, the analysis is limited to executions occurring under state jurisdiction and excludes executions occurring under local jurisdictions and federal jurisdiction. Prior to the 1940s, executions under local jurisdictions were common, comprising three-quarters of all executions prior to 1945. However, the Epsy data from local jurisdictions comes from newspaper accounts and other disparate sources and is known to be an incomplete source of local capital punishment data. The state data, however, is mostly complete.


The Epsy data is also less complete during earlier years. I focused particularly on data from 1864 to present because of work Negley K. Teeters and Charles J. Zibulka conducted that recorded state executions from 1864 to 1967. Epsy confirmed all of the findings from Teeters and Zibulka, meaning that all of the data reported until 2002 should have been checked by at least two separate sources, making the overall data recorded fairly robust and complete.


Finally, there were some disagreements between the Epsy data and the data from the Death Penalty Information Center. For every year in which the number of executions reported by Epsy differed from the Death Penalty Information Center figures, the Death Penalty Information Center numbers were greater. This suggests that the data from the Death Penalty Information Center is slightly more complete than the Epsy data. Therefore, I chose to use the Death Penalty Information Center data from 1976 - 2018, excluding 2019 data from the Death Penalty Information Center and all of the Epsy data occurring on or after 1976.
